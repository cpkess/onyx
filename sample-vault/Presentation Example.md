# Presentation Example

## General Advice for Producing a Presentation: Before You Begin

- The content in this Powerpoint is intended as a general guide – there are many ways to make an effective slideshow, and you may approach it differently depending on your subject.

> [!tip] Flexibility in Approach
> The content is intended as a general guide, allowing for various approaches depending on your specific subject matter.

- Check to see if there are any specific guidelines in the module handbook. Do you need to include anything specific, or use a particular font? Always prioritise fulfilling any assignment-specific guidelines.

> [!important] Prioritize Guidelines
> Always prioritize fulfilling any specific guidelines mentioned in the module handbook, especially regarding inclusions or font usage.

- Think about your audience. Why are you giving this presentation, and who are you giving it to?

> [!question] Audience Context
> Determine the purpose of your presentation and identify your audience to tailor the content effectively.

- Are they subject experts (e.g. a tutor, or someone studying on your degree) or someone who is learning about this content for the first time?

> [!note] Audience Type Identification
> Assess whether your audience consists of subject experts (like a tutor or fellow student) or novices.

- What do you need to include for them to understand your point, and what do they already know?

> [!tip] Content Tailoring
> Determine precisely what information is necessary for the audience's comprehension based on their existing knowledge level.

## General Advice for Producing a Presentation: While Writing

> [!tip] Focus on Key Points
> Try to focus on key points to avoid overwhelming your slides with text, and make use of bullet points to break text up.

> [!note] Handling Slide Content
> You will not read directly from your slides, so including key points and expanding on these is enough.

> [!tip] Managing Slide Volume
> Think about how many slides to include. Try not to include too few (e.g., three slides for a 1-hour talk) or too many (e.g., 50 slides for a 10-minute talk).

> [!tip] Pacing Strategy
> A useful starting point may be to aim for one slide per minute of talking, though this is heavily dependent on your subject and the purpose of the presentation.

> [!tip] Structuring Flow
> Decide the order of your slides based on what you want to tell your audience – what order will make the content clear and easy to follow for them?

## General Advice for Producing a Presentation: Advice on Visuals

### Advice on Visuals

> [!important] Image Clarity and Permissions
> Ensure any images you include are clear and relevant to the content. Remember to make sure you have permission to use them.

> [!warning] Avoid Extreme Color Combinations
> Avoid using extreme colour combinations—for example, bright yellow text is difficult to read, and if you use a lot of clashing colours on a slide (e.g., green, red, bright orange and neon purple) it can become visually overwhelming.

> [!tip] Prioritize Simplicity
> Although it can be tempting to show off your design skills, simpler and easy to read is usually better. Avoid distracting transitions, animations, and fancy fonts, unless these are relevant to your course.

> [!note] Utilize White Space
> Remember to include white space—don’t overwhelm your slides with excessive text, tiny text, or an extreme number of images.

> [!tip] Ensure Accessibility
> Use the accessibility checker on PowerPoint, or other accessibility checkers available online, to ensure that the presentation is visually accessible.

## Advice for Your Title Slide

> [!tip] Image Selection
> Select an image which is clear (check that the image is not pixelated/unclear if you resize it) and related to the topic you are presenting on.

> [!tip] Title/Subtitle Clarity
> Choose a title/subtitle which accurately reflects the content of your presentation, and helps to indicate to your reader what information is coming.

> [!tip] Readability Check
> Check to make sure your title is readable against the image.

> [!question] Required Information Check
> Check in case your school/module guide has a style guide or any advice – do you need to include your name, student number and module code on your title slide?

## Example Basic Content Slide: Themes in Jane Austen’s Novels

> [!insight] Core Themes in Austen's Novels
> Austen's novels generally focus on the following interconnected themes:
> *   Social class
> *   Love and marriage
> *   Family relationships
> *   Wealth (or lack thereof)
> *   Friendship

> [!note] Parody and Reality in Literature
> Works like *Northanger Abbey* use literary devices to explore thematic gaps:
> *   It functions as a parody of Gothic fiction.
> *   It includes themes concerning the gap between fantasy and reality.

> [!tip] Structuring Presentations
> You might want to include these components to split up your presentation into themed sections, such as: Problem, Hypothesis, Investigation, Results, and Analysis.
